Aircrafts And Planes

Airplanes are struck by lightning relatively frequently (about once per year per aircraft), but modern aircraft are designed with conductive materials to safely channel the electricity around the cabin. Engine redundancy: Many commercial airplanes can safely fly on one engine, and even if both fail, they can glide for a significant distance. Altitude: Commercial airplanes typically cruise at altitudes between 30,000 and 42,000 feet (9.14 to 12.8 km) for optimal fuel efficiency and to avoid most weather turbulence. Tires: Airplane tires are incredibly durable, able to withstand the impact of landing and the pressure of supporting the plane's weight. Pilot and co-pilot meals: To prevent food poisoning from affecting both pilots, they are required to eat different meals.

Commercial jetliners typically cruise at speeds between 480 and 575 mph (770-930 km/h). Fastest plane: The Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird is the fastest plane ever built, capable of reaching speeds of 2,193 mph (3,529 km/h). Largest plane: The Stratolaunch, with a wingspan longer than a football field, is the largest airplane ever built.
